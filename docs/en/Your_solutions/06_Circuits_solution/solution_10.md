# Task 10 – Average Current of a Lightning Bolt

## Problem Statement

A lightning bolt transfers a charge of $30\,\text{C}$ to the ground in a time of $2\,\text{ms}$. Calculate the average current of the lightning bolt.

## Theory

The average current ($I_{avg}$) is defined as the total amount of charge ($Q$) passing through a cross-section of a conductor divided by the total time interval ($\Delta t$) during which the transfer occurs:

$$
I_{avg} = \frac{ Q}{\Delta t}
$$

When working with physics problems, it is essential to convert all units to the International System of Units (SI):
- Charge is measured in Coulombs ($\text{C}$).
- Time is measured in seconds ($\text{s}$).

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Unit Conversion
The time is given in milliseconds ($\text{ms}$). We must convert this to seconds:

$$
\Delta t = 2\,\text{ms} = 2 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{s} = 0.002\,\text{s}
$$

### 2. Calculate Average Current
Using the formula for average current:

$$
\begin{align}
I_{avg} &= \frac{30\,\text{C}}{0.002\,\text{s}} \\
        &= \frac{30}{2 \times 10^{-3}} \\
        &= 15,000\text{A}
\end{align}
$$

Converting to a more standard notation:

$$
I_{avg} = 15,000\,\text{A} 
$$

## Final Result

$$
I_{avg} = 15,000\text{A}
$$

## Interpretation
A current of $15,000\,\text{Amperes}$ is exceptionally high compared to household circuits (which typically handle $10\text{--}16\,\text{A}$). This high current, even over a very short duration, is what causes the extreme heat and explosive expansion of air (thunder) associated with lightning.

# Task 01 – Wave Properties

## Problem Statement

A sound wave in air has a frequency of $440 \text{ Hz}$. If the speed of sound in air is $343 \text{ m/s}$, what is its wavelength? What is its wavelength in water, where the speed of sound is $1482 \text{ m/s}$?

## Theory

The fundamental relationship between the speed of a wave, its frequency, and its wavelength is given by the wave propagation equation:

$$
v = f \lambda
$$

Where:
* $v$ is the phase velocity of the wave (m/s).
* $f$ is the frequency (Hz or 1/s).
* $\lambda$ is the wavelength (m).

It is important to note that when a wave moves from one medium to another (e.g., from air to water), its **frequency remains constant** because it is determined by the source. The velocity and wavelength change proportionally.

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Calculation for Air
Given:
* $f = 440 \text{ Hz}$
* $v_{\text{air}} = 343 \text{ m/s}$

We rearrange the wave equation to solve for wavelength $\lambda_{\text{air}}$:

$$
\lambda_{\text{air}} = \frac{v_{\text{air}}}{f}
$$

Substituting the values:

$$
\lambda_{\text{air}} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}} \approx 0.7795 \text{ m}
$$

### 2. Calculation for Water
Given:
* $f = 440 \text{ Hz}$ (remains unchanged)
* $v_{\text{water}} = 1482 \text{ m/s}$

We solve for wavelength $\lambda_{\text{water}}$:

$$
\lambda_{\text{water}} = \frac{v_{\text{water}}}{f}
$$

Substituting the values:

$$
\lambda_{\text{water}} = \frac{1482 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}} \approx 3.3682 \text{ m}
$$

## Final Result

The wavelength of the sound wave is:
* In air: $\lambda \approx 0.78 \text{ m}$
* In water: $\lambda \approx 3.37 \text{ m}$

## Interpretation

The frequency of $440 \text{ Hz}$ corresponds to the musical note A4. As the wave enters water, it travels significantly faster (over 4 times the speed in air). Since the frequency is fixed by the source, the spatial extent of each wave cycle (the wavelength) must increase to account for the higher speed.
